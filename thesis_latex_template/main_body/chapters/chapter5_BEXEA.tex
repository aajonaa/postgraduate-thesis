\section{Introduction}
Expensive optimization problems (EOPs) refer to problems in which objective functions or constraints are costly to evaluate. Real-world examples include airfoil optimization using computational fluid dynamics simulations \cite{liu2023airfoils} and vehicle crashworthiness optimization using finite element simulations \cite{kumar2024optimization}, where each evaluation requires significant computational time. As a result, only a few hundred or thousand function evaluations are feasible. However, traditional evolutionary algorithms (EAs) typically require tens of thousands of function evaluations to obtain acceptable solutions \cite{he2023review}. To address this challenge, surrogate-assisted evolutionary algorithms (SAEAs) have gained significant research interest by integrating machine learning models into traditional EAs \cite{kudela2022recent}.

SAEAs typically begin with a fixed sample archive, where the real objective function is used to evaluate the fitness of the initial samples. A surrogate model is then trained based on this archive, and most offspring generated by the search strategy are evaluated using this model, which provides predicted fitness values. This approach reduces the number of exact function evaluations \cite{jin2018data}, preserving evaluations for promising candidates and thereby decreasing the overall computational cost of the optimization process. Common surrogate models used in SAEAs include the radial basis function (RBF) \cite{regis2013evolutionary, sun2017surrogate}, the kriging model (also known as the Gaussian process model) \cite{buche2005accelerating, liu2013gaussian}, support vector machines (SVM) \cite{loshchilov2010comparison, guo2018heterogeneous}, and polynomial response surfaces (PRS) \cite{zhou2005study, chavez2016polynomial}. Each model has distinct parameters and characteristics, making them suitable for different problem domains.

According to a recent study \cite{he2023review}, the RBF model has gained popularity due to its adaptability, while the Kriging model is the most widely used in Bayesian optimization frameworks. In contrast, SVM and PRS are less prioritized in SAEA research for expensive optimization. The primary reason for this trend lies in the RBF model's low complexity and fast prediction capability. Although Kriging has a high training cost, its uncertainty quantification is highly beneficial for balancing exploration and exploitation in SAEAs. Conversely, SVM suffers from both high training and prediction costs and lacks uncertainty estimation, limiting its applicability. PRS, on the other hand, is highly sensitive to problem dimensionality, performing efficiently only in low-dimensional settings while struggling with nonlinear problems. Therefore, in this work, we adopt the RBF model in our BEXEA framework.

In addition to the surrogate model in the SAEA framework, the search strategy is another critical component responsible for generating offspring that are prescreened by the model. Differential Evolution (DE) has emerged as one of the most influential and frequently used optimizers in SAEA research \cite{khaldi2024surrogate} due to its robust performance, compatibility with surrogate models, and adaptability to diverse problem types, including low-dimensional, high-dimensional, single-objective, and multi-objective optimization. However, the effectiveness of DE is constrained by the design of its scaling factor and crossover rate, which can limit search efficiency, particularly in high-dimensional spaces.

In recent years, efforts have been made to address these limitations. For example, \cite{stanovov2023surrogate} employs a Taylor series to model the relationship between DE parameters and algorithm performance, enabling automated parameter adaptation. Similarly, \cite{mallipeddi2015evolving} integrates a Kriging model into the SAEA framework while dynamically adjusting DE parameters based on the model's representation of the search basin at different evolutionary stages. Although these approaches eliminate the need for manual parameter tuning, they rely on the assumption that the surrogate model accurately reflects the fitness landscape, which is often not the case, particularly in high-dimensional and noisy settings.

Another challenge in the search strategy of SAEAs is balancing exploration and exploitation. A common and practical solution adopted in many SAEAs is to construct multiple surrogate models by partitioning the decision space into multiple subspaces using the K-means algorithm \cite{lv2023multi, yu2025surrogate}. In this approach, a global model focuses on exploration to maintain diversity, while a local model refines promising solutions to accelerate convergence. The specific surrogate models used for global and local searches can vary based on the problem characteristics. Another strategy involves employing different evolutionary optimization algorithms for distinct purposes. For instance, \cite{li2020surrogate} selects the parameter-free Teaching-Learning-Based Optimization (TLBO) for global search and Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) for exploitation. Additionally, a recent study replaced the local search component of PSO with Differential Evolution (DE) \cite{lin2024surrogate}, achieving competitive performance on 35 benchmark functions. While manually designed exploration and exploitation strategies are effective for EOPs, their adaptability may be limited to the test problems.

After offspring are generated by the optimizers and prescreened by the surrogate model, infill criteria are employed to select one or more candidate solutions from the offspring for exact function evaluations. The most popular criteria currently used in SAEAs include Expected Improvement (EI) \cite{mockus1994application}, Lower Confidence Bound (LCB) \cite{cox1992statistical}, Expected Hypervolume Improvement (EHVI) \cite{daulton2020differentiable}, and Probability of Improvement (PI) \cite{viana2010surrogate}. EI, originating from Efficient Global Optimization (EGO) \cite{jones1998efficient}, is the most widely used criterion in SAEAs, as it incorporates both the predicted value from the surrogate model and its associated uncertainty. EHVI extends the concept of EI by measuring the expected increase in the hypervolume of the Pareto front, making it popular in multi-objective SAEAs \cite{pang2022expensive}. Recently, LCB has gained popularity as an alternative to EI, due to its effective management of the trade-off between exploration and exploitation \cite{li2023elite}. Although PI is less commonly used than EI, it is still employed in some SAEAs due to its simplicity. However, a common drawback of these criteria is that, while they provide benefits when sampling points from the offspring, the underlying rationale for the criteria's scores remains unclear.

In this work, we address two key issues: the labor-intensive manual design of exploration and exploitation strategies, and the lack of explainability in uncertainty criteria. To solve the first problem, we propose a two-arm bandit-driven \cite{slivkins2019introduction} adaptive search, which alternately adjusts its search mode based on environmental feedback through reinforcement learning. For the second issue, we incorporate an explainability component derived from feature importance, inspired by the SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) principle \cite{nohara2022explanation}, into the EI criterion. This approach balances optimization performance with explainability, preferring points with clear rationales.

The major contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*)]
  \item Designing a bandit-driven adaptive search framework for SAEAs, which automatically adjusts its search mode based on environmental feedback.
  \item Formulating an explainable uncertainty criterion that improves performance by sampling candidate solutions.
  \item Proposing BEXEA as part of the SAEAs family and verifying its advantages through comparisons with seven state-of-the-art SAEAs on two complex benchmark sets and a real-world problem.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this article is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the related techniques of evolutionary sampling strategies, the RBF model, and the EI criterion. Section 3 describes the proposed BEXEA, and Section 4 presents the numerical experiments and results. Conclusions are provided in Section 5.

\section{Related Techniques}

For ease of understanding, we briefly introduce the three components of BEXEA. First, we discuss the evolutionary algorithm sampling strategies, including the Estimation of Distribution Algorithm (EDA) and Quantum-behaved Particle Swarm Optimization (QPSO). Second, we describe the RBF surrogate model, which assists in offspring prescreening. Finally, we present a fundamental element of this study: the  infill sampling criterion.

\subsection{EA}
\subsubsection{EDA}

Recently, the EDA algorithm has demonstrated effective search capabilities by estimating the distribution of promising solutions through a probability model \cite{chen2020language}, \cite{liu2023surrogate}. Unlike traditional genetic operators, EDA generates offspring by fitting the input population $\mathcal{P}=\{\boldsymbol{x}_1,\boldsymbol{x}_2,...,\boldsymbol{x}_N\}$ (where each $\boldsymbol{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, with $d$ representing the problem's dimensionality) and their corresponding fitness values $\mathcal{F}=\{f(\boldsymbol{x}_1),f(\boldsymbol{x}_2),...,f(\boldsymbol{x}_N)\}$ to form a distribution model. In this work, the population is modeled using a multivariate Gaussian distribution, characterized by a mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\mathcal{C}ov}$, which are obtained as follows.

\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\mu} = \frac{1}{N}\sum\limits_{i=1}^{N}\boldsymbol{x}_i
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\mathcal{C}ov} = \frac{1}{N-1}\sum\limits_{i=1}^N(\boldsymbol{x}_i-\boldsymbol{\mu})(\boldsymbol{x}_i-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T
\end{equation}

After the model is fitted, a new population will be sampling from the Gaussian distribution $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu},\boldsymbol{\mathcal{C}ov})$.

\subsubsection{QPSO}
Another adopted sampling strategy in this paper is QPSO, a variant of PSO, the incorporated quantum mechanism modelling particles position probabilitically instead of deterministically enhancing the convergence speed while reduce the computation time \cite{sun2012quantum}, \cite{li2025multi}. Most commonly employed in the computationally expensive optimization assisted with surrogate model, it eliminate velocity uses a quantum-inspired position update based on a potential well centered at the local attrator.

For the particle position update, we first determine the personal best positions up to the current iteration, and then identify the global best position among the personal best positions, as follows:

\begin{equation}
    pbest_i = \mathop{\rm arg\,min}_{\tau \leq t} f(\boldsymbol{x}_i^\tau)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    gbest = \mathop{\rm arg\,min}_i f(pbest_i)
\end{equation}
where $\tau = 1$ indicates the best position for particle $i$ over all iterations from the start up to the current iteration $t$.

After determining the personal and global best positions of the particles, we calculate the local attractor for each particle $i$ at dimension $j$:

\begin{equation}
    p_{ij} = \varphi_{ij} \cdot pbest_{ij} + (1-\varphi_{ij}) \cdot gbest_j, \quad \varphi_{ij} \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)
\end{equation}

Based on the local attractor, we sample the particle position from a quantum probability distribution:

\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{x}_{ij}^{t+1} = p_{ij} \pm \alpha \left| mbest_j - \boldsymbol{x}_{ij}^t \right| \cdot \ln \left(\frac{1}{u_{ij}} \right), \quad u_{ij} \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)
\end{equation}
where $mbest = \frac{1}{N} \sum\limits_{i=1}^N pbest_i$ is the mean best, the $\pm$ is chosen randomly with a probability of 0.5, and $\alpha$ is the contraction-expansion coefficient that linearly decreases from 1 to 0.5 over iterations.

\subsection{RBF Model}

The RBF model starts with a fixed number of samples $\mathcal{P}$ that are exactly evaluated. The obtained fitness values $\mathcal{F}$ and the samples are then used to fit the model. The RBF approximates the fitness landscape based on the input samples. The interpolation form of the RBF model is given as follows:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{f} (\boldsymbol{x}_i) = \sum\limits_{j=1}^N \omega_j \psi(\left|| \boldsymbol{x}_i - \boldsymbol{x}_j \right||)
\end{equation}
where $\hat{f}(\cdot)$ is the approximated function of the RBF, $\psi(\cdot)$ denotes the basis function, and $\mathcal{W} = \{\omega_1, \omega_2, \dots, \omega_N\}$ are the weight coefficients of the basis functions. Commonly used basis functions include cubic functions, Gaussian functions, linear splines, and multiquadric splines. In this work, we adopt the multiquadric splines, such that $\psi(\left|| \boldsymbol{x}_i - \boldsymbol{x}_j \right||) = \sqrt{1 + (\left|| \boldsymbol{x}_i - \boldsymbol{x}_j \right||)^2}$.

To determine the coefficients $\mathcal{W}$, the RBF model must satisfy the interpolation condition:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x}_i) = f(\boldsymbol{x}_i),
\end{equation}
which leads to:

\begin{equation}
    f(\boldsymbol{x}_i) = \sum\limits_{i=1}^N \omega_i \psi(\left|| \boldsymbol{x} - \boldsymbol{x}_i \right||),
\end{equation}
By formatting this as a matrix, we get:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F} = \boldsymbol{\Psi} \mathcal{W}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\Psi}$ is the kernel matrix with elements $\boldsymbol{\Psi}_{ij} = \psi(\left|| \boldsymbol{x}_i - \boldsymbol{x}_j \right||)$, and the coefficients $\mathcal{W}$ can be obtained by solving this linear system.

Fig. \ref{fig:bexea_RBF_prediction} shows the RBF approximation of a 1-dimensional function using six uniformly distributed samples \cite{forrester2008engineering}. The white circles indicate the sample points in the space, the solid black line represents the real fitness landscape, and the red dashed line represents the RBF predicted function. From this figure, we can see that the RBF model interpolates the sample points, with the estimated error being zero at the sample points and increasing between them.

\subsection{EI Criterion}

After the model is built, the next step is to determine which sample should be evaluated with the expensive objective function. This strategy of screening the offspring is often referred to as infill sampling criteria. Among the criteria used in SAEAs, EI is the most widely employed and studied \cite{zhan2020expected}. It estimates the expected improvement of a new candidate over the best value observed. For a minimization problem, the EI at point $\boldsymbol{x}$ is defined as follows:

\begin{equation}
    \text{EI}(\boldsymbol{x}) = \mathbb{E}[\max(\mathcal{F}_\text{best} - \hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x}), 0)]
\end{equation}

To transition from the integral form of EI to a closed-form expression, we assume the surrogate model provides a Gaussian predictive distribution for $f(\boldsymbol{x})$, characterized by a mean $\mu(\boldsymbol{x})$ and standard deviation $\sigma(\boldsymbol{x})$. The EI can then be expressed in closed form as:

\begin{equation}
    \text{EI} = (\mathcal{F}_\text{best} - \mu(\boldsymbol{x})) \cdot \Phi \left( \frac{\mathcal{F}_\text{best} - \mu(\boldsymbol{x})}{\sigma(\boldsymbol{x})} \right) + \sigma(\boldsymbol{x}) \cdot \phi \left( \frac{\mathcal{F}_\text{best} - \mu(\boldsymbol{x})}{\sigma(\boldsymbol{x})} \right)
\end{equation}
where $\Phi$ and $\phi$ denote the cumulative distribution function and probability density function of the standard normal distribution, respectively.

\section{BEXEA}
\subsection{Algorithm Framework}

The overall BEXEA algorithm is structured into an initialization phase and a main loop that continues until the maximum number of function evaluations is reached. In the initialization phase, an initial population is generated via Latin hypercube sampling, evaluated using the true objective function, and stored in an archive. An RBF surrogate model is built from the current population, and the bandit reward estimates $Q(a)$ for each search strategy (EDA and QPSO) are initialized to zero. The exploration rate $\epsilon$, decay factor $\beta$, and stagnation counter are also set at this stage.

Within the main loop, the algorithm first updates the surrogate model with the current population. Strategy selection is then performed using an $\epsilon$-greedy mechanism: if stagnation is detected (i.e., no improvement occurs for $\mathcal{S}$ consecutive iterations), the exploration rate is increased, and a random strategy is selected. Otherwise, the strategy with the highest estimated $Q$ value is chosen with probability $1-\epsilon$ (with a random strategy chosen with probability $\epsilon$). The selected strategy is then applied to generate offspring solutions.

Each offspring is first ranked using a combined score that incorporates both its predicted fitness (from the surrogate model) and the Explainable Uncertainty (EU) criterion (detailed in Algorithm~\ref{alg:bexea_EU}). A limited number of top-ranked offspring are then evaluated using the true objective function. For each evaluated candidate, an immediate reward is computed based on fitness improvement, enhanced by a strategy-specific measure (diversity for EDA or EU for QPSO), and used to update the bandit estimate $Q(a)$ via an exponential recency-weighted average. The population is updated if an evaluated candidate improves upon the current worst solution, and the stagnation counter is adjusted accordingly.

This high-level structure ensures that the algorithm continuously balances exploration and exploitation: the adaptive bandit mechanism favors strategies with better historical performance, while the EU criterion guides offspring ranking by accounting for both fitness improvement and interpretability. The following high-level pseudocode in Algorithm~\ref{alg:bexea_BEXEA} summarizes the overall BEXEA algorithm, assisted by the algorithm structure diagram presented in Fig.~\ref{fig:bexea_BEXEA}, while the detailed procedures for the MAB and EU components are provided in Algorithms~\ref{alg:bexea_MAB} and \ref{alg:bexea_EU}.

\subsection{Multi-armed Bandit Driven Adaptive Search}
Studies and practical applications indicate that MAB approaches offer advantages over static or uniform strategy selection methods in optimization frameworks \cite{xie2023surrogate}, \cite{gong2024surrogate}. Three aspects are noteworthy: 1) MAB methods provide a theoretical basis for balancing exploration and exploitation, reducing regret and supporting convergence; 2) they manage dynamic decision spaces, particularly when selecting among various evolutionary strategies, thus avoiding the need for exhaustive evaluation; 3) they lessen reliance on suboptimal strategies by adaptively learning from performance feedback. In this work, we integrate an epsilon-greedy MAB into SAEA algorithm to enable adaptive selection between the EDA and QPSO strategies. The details of the epsilon-greedy MAB with stagnation-driven adjustments are described as follows.

In many optimization algorithms, different search strategies often excel at different stages of the optimization process. We model the strategy selection problem as a $\mathcal{A}$-armed bandit problem, where each arm $i \in \{1, 2, \ldots, \mathcal{A}\}$ represents a distinct search strategy. In our implementation, we utilize $\mathcal{A}=2$ strategies, including EDA and QPSO as described in Section 2.

At each iteration $t$, the algorithm must select a strategy $a_t \in \{1, 2, \ldots, \mathcal{A}\}$ to generate offspring solutions. After evaluating these solutions, a reward $r_t$ is computed, which is used to update the estimated value $Q_t(a)$ of each strategy.

We employ an $\epsilon$-greedy strategy selection mechanism:

\begin{equation}
a_t =
\begin{cases}
\mathop{\rm arg\,max}\limits_a Q_t(a) & \text{with probability } 1-\epsilon \\
\text{random strategy} & \text{with probability } \epsilon
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon \in [0.1,0.3]$ controls the exploration-exploitation trade-off. This mechanism ensures that the algorithm primarily exploits the currently best-performing strategy while occasionally exploring alternatives.

The reward for a strategy is computed based on the improvement it achieves. For a newly evaluated solution $\boldsymbol{x}$ with fitness $f(\boldsymbol{x})$, the immediate reward is:

\begin{equation}
r_{\text{imm}} = \frac{\max(0, \mathcal{F}_\text{min} - f(\boldsymbol{x}))}{\mathcal{F}_\text{min} + \epsilon_{\text{small}}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{F}_{min}$ is the previous best fitness and $\epsilon_{\text{small}}$ is a small constant to prevent division by zero.

We enhance this basic reward with strategy-specific components:

\begin{equation}
r_t =
\begin{cases}
r_{\text{imm}} + \alpha \cdot \frac{\mathcal{D}iv}{d} & \text{for EDA strategy} \\
r_{\text{imm}} + \alpha \cdot \text{EU}(\boldsymbol{x}) & \text{for QPSO strategy}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is a scaling factor (set to 0.1), $\mathcal{D}iv$ is the minimum distance from the new solution to existing population members, representing the diversity measure, and $\text{EU}(\boldsymbol{x})$ is the EU criteria value for the new solution.

To balance recent performance with historical trends, we employ an exponential recency-weighted average for strategy value estimation:

\begin{equation}
Q_{t+1}(a_t) = (1-\beta) \cdot Q_t(a_t) + \beta \cdot r_t
\end{equation}
where $\beta \in [0,1]$ is a decay factor (set to 0.3) that determines the weight given to recent rewards versus historical performance.

The MAB framework incorporates an adaptive mechanism to handle stagnation. When no improvement is found for $\mathcal{S}$ consecutive iterations (denoted as stagnation count, initially set to 20), the algorithm increases exploration by: 1) Forcing random strategy selection for one iteration; 2) Increasing the exploration rate: $\epsilon_{t+1} = \min(\epsilon_{\text{max}}, \epsilon_t \cdot \gamma)$, where $\epsilon_{\text{max}}$ is the maximum exploration rate (0.3) and $\gamma$ is the growth factor (1.1).

This adaptive mechanism allows the algorithm to escape local optima by temporarily increasing exploration when progress stagnates, while maintaining efficient exploitation during periods of steady improvement.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:bexea_MAB} presents the pseudocode for the MAB framework in BEXEA, and Fig.~\ref{fig:bexea_MAB} illustrates its workflow. Beginning with initialization (all strategy values $Q(a)$ set to zero), the algorithm cycles through decision-making, performance evaluation, and updates. When stagnation is detected (i.e., the count reaches $\mathcal{S}$), the exploration rate increases and a random strategy is chosen; otherwise, the $\epsilon$-greedy rule selects the strategy. This adaptive process enhances both convergence efficiency and solution quality.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Multi-Armed Bandit Adaptive Search in BEXEA}\label{alg:bexea_MAB}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Strategies $\mathcal{A}$, exploration rate $\epsilon$, decay $\beta$, stagnation threshold $\mathcal{S}$
    \State \textbf{Initialize:} $Q(a) \gets 0$, $\epsilon$, stagnation count $\gets 0$

    \For{each iteration $t$}
        \State Select $a_t$:
        \Indent If stagnation $\geq \mathcal{S}$, increase $\epsilon$, choose random $a_t$
        \Indent Else, apply $\epsilon$-greedy selection
        \State Generate solution $\boldsymbol{x}$ using $a_t$ and evaluate $f(\boldsymbol{x})$
        \State Compute reward $r_t$ based on $r_{\text{imm}}$
        \State Update $Q(a_t) \gets (1 - \beta) Q(a_t) + \beta r_t$
        \State Reset or increment stagnation count based on improvement
    \EndFor

    \State \textbf{Output:} Updated strategy values $Q(a)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Explainable Uncertainty Criteria}
The EU criteria combines concepts from Bayesian optimization and explainable AI to provide an uncertainty measure that balances exploration and exploitation while maintaining interpretability.

For each candidate solution $\boldsymbol{x}$, we identify the $\mathcal{K}$ nearest solutions in the training data $\mathcal{D} = \{(\boldsymbol{x}_i, f(\boldsymbol{x}_i))\}_{i=1}^N$ based on Euclidean distance:

\begin{equation}
dist(\boldsymbol{x}, \boldsymbol{x}_i) = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^d (\boldsymbol{x}_j - \boldsymbol{x}_{ij})^2}
\end{equation}

Let $\mathcal{I}_\mathcal{K}(\boldsymbol{x}) = \{i_1, i_2, \ldots, i_\mathcal{K}\}$ represent the indices of the $\mathcal{K}$ nearest neighbors to $\boldsymbol{x}$. Using these neighbors, we estimate the local mean and standard deviation:

\begin{equation}
\hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x}) = \frac{1}{\mathcal{K}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_\mathcal{K}(\boldsymbol{x})} f(\boldsymbol{x}_i)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\hat{\sigma}(\boldsymbol{x}) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{\mathcal{K}-1} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_\mathcal{K}(\boldsymbol{x})} (f(\boldsymbol{x}_i) - \hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x}))^2}
\end{equation}

Using the estimated mean and standard deviation, we calculate the EI as:

\begin{equation}
\text{EI}(\boldsymbol{x}) =
\begin{cases}
(\hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x}) - \mathcal{F}_\text{min}) \cdot \Phi(z) + \hat{\sigma}(\boldsymbol{x}) \cdot \phi(z) & \text{if } \hat{\sigma}(\boldsymbol{x}) > 0 \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{F}_\text{min}$ is the best fitness value found so far, $z = \frac{\hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x}) - \mathcal{F}_\text{min}}{\hat{\sigma}(\boldsymbol{x})}$, $\Phi(\cdot)$ is the cumulative distribution function of the standard normal distribution, and $\phi(\cdot)$ is the probability density function of the standard normal distribution.

Note that in a typical EI formulation, we would have $\mathcal{F}_\text{best} - \hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x})$ in the numerator for $z$, but our formulation is adapted for minimization problems and handles the case where $\hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x}) > \mathcal{F}_\text{best}$.

For each feature dimension $j \in \{1, 2, \ldots, d\}$, we calculate the absolute difference between the candidate solution and the mean of its nearest neighbors:

\begin{equation}
\Delta_j(\boldsymbol{x}) = \left| \boldsymbol{x}_j - \frac{1}{\mathcal{K}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_\mathcal{K}(\boldsymbol{x})} \boldsymbol{x}_{i,j} \right|
\end{equation}

These differences approximate local feature importance, inspired by SHAP values. We normalize these differences to create a probability distribution over features:

\begin{equation}
\mathbb{P}_j(\boldsymbol{x}) =
\begin{cases}
\frac{\Delta_j(\boldsymbol{x})}{\sum_{j=1}^d \Delta_{j}(\boldsymbol{x})} & \text{if } \sum_{j=1}^d \Delta_{j}(\boldsymbol{x}) > 0 \\
\frac{1}{d} & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Using the feature importance distribution, we calculate Shannon entropy as a measure of uncertainty:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H}(\boldsymbol{x}) = -\sum_{j=1}^d \mathbb{P}_j(\boldsymbol{x}) \log_2(\mathbb{P}_j(\boldsymbol{x}) + \epsilon_\text{small})
\end{equation}
where $\epsilon$ is a small constant to prevent the logarithm of zero. Higher entropy indicates greater uncertainty about which features are driving the prediction.

The final Explainable Uncertainty criteria combines the EI with the entropy-based uncertainty measure:

\begin{equation}
\text{EU}(\boldsymbol{x}) = \frac{\text{EI}(\boldsymbol{x})}{1 + \mathcal{H}(\boldsymbol{x})}
\end{equation}

This formulation has several desirable properties: 1) Points with high expected improvement are favored; 2) Points with low entropy (clear feature attributions) are preferred over those with high entropy; 3) The criteria is interpretable: high values indicate promising regions where we can explain why they are promising.

In the BEXEA algorithm, the EU criteria is used in two ways: 1) For candidate solution ranking: $\text{score}(\boldsymbol{x}) = \hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x}) + \lambda \cdot (\mathcal{EU}_{\text{max}} - \text{EU}(\boldsymbol{x}))$; 2) As a component of the reward function for the multi-armed bandit, where $\lambda$ is a scaling factor (set to 0.5), and $\mathcal{EU}_{\text{max}}$ is the maximum EU value among all candidates.

This integration of the EU criteria enables the algorithm to balance between promising regions (high EI) and regions where the surrogate model's predictions have clear, attributable causes (low entropy), enhancing both the efficiency and interpretability of the optimization process.

This structured representation provides a clear step-by-step procedure for integrating the EU criteria into optimization frameworks, ensuring both computational efficiency and interpretability in decision-making processes.

Fig.~\ref{fig:bexea_EU} illustrates the key components of the EU criteria, showing how it balances exploitation (through EI) with interpretability (through entropy-based feature importance). As depicted, the $\mathcal{K}$-nearest neighbors of a candidate solution are used to estimate both the predicted value $\hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x})$ and uncertainty $\hat{\sigma}(\boldsymbol{x})$, while feature importance probabilities are calculated to determine which dimensions most significantly influence the prediction. This dual approach enables the algorithm to prioritize promising regions where the surrogate model's predictions have clear, attributable causes, thus enhancing both the efficiency and explainability of the optimization process.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Computation of Explainable Uncertainty Criteria}\label{alg:bexea_EU}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Training data $\mathcal{D} = \{(\boldsymbol{x}_i, f(\boldsymbol{x}_i))\}_{i=1}^N$, test points $\mathcal{X}_{\text{test}}$, parameter $\mathcal{K}$
    \State \textbf{Initialize:} $\mathcal{F}_{\text{min}} = \min(f(\boldsymbol{x}_i))$, output vector $\mathcal{O}$

    \For{each test point $\boldsymbol{x} \in \mathcal{X}_{\text{test}}$}
        \State Identify $\mathcal{K}$ nearest neighbors of $\boldsymbol{x}$ in $\mathcal{D}$
        \State Compute predicted mean $\hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x})$ and standard deviation $\hat{\sigma}(\boldsymbol{x})$
        \State Compute EI using $\hat{f}(\boldsymbol{x})$, $\mathcal{F}_{\text{min}}$, and $\hat{\sigma}(\boldsymbol{x})$
        \State Compute feature importance probabilities $\mathbb{P}_j(\boldsymbol{x})$ from neighbors
        \State Compute entropy $\mathcal{H}(\boldsymbol{x})$ of the feature importance distribution
        \State Compute $\text{EU}(\boldsymbol{x}) = \frac{\text{EI}(\boldsymbol{x})}{1 + \mathcal{H}(\boldsymbol{x})}$ and store in $\mathcal{O}$
    \EndFor

    \State \textbf{Output:} Explainable Uncertainty vector $\mathcal{O}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Effectiveness of Adaptive Search}
The multi-armed bandit-driven adaptive search addresses the issue of manually or specifically designing the exploration and exploitation phases in existing SAEA frameworks for exploring the solution space. The adaptive search uses a history performance metric to calculate the reward, akin to reinforcement learning, guiding the search for new candidates based on the feedback from the current population's environment. This approach avoids the need to manually choose the exploration or exploitation operator or design parameters based on the optimization process. In this study, we selected two arms: EDA as arm 1 ($a1$) and QPSO as arm 2 ($a2$). Thus, BEXEA variants such as BEXEA-no-a1 refer to the algorithm using QPSO without EDA, while BEXEA-no-a2 refers to the algorithm using EDA without QPSO. The experiment analyzes the performance of these three algorithms to determine the influence and contribution of these two arms to the proposed BEXEA. The experimental results are shown in Table~\ref{t:bexea_MAB_Table}, accompanied by the Friedman ranking and Wilcoxon signed-rank test results at the bottom of the table.

Upon examining the Wilcoxon signed-rank test results, we see that BEXEA outperforms BEXEA-no-a1 on 8 functions, while only outperforming BEXEA-no-a2 on 3 functions. Additionally, there are 8 competitive performance functions for BEXEA-no-a2, while only 4 for BEXEA-no-a1. This indicates that BEXEA-no-a2 has relatively worse optimization performance compared to BEXEA-no-a1, showing that the arm 2 strategy (QPSO) has a significant impact on BEXEA. Therefore, arm 2 (QPSO) contributes more to the superior optimization performance than arm 1 (EDA). The combined strategy of arm 1 and arm 2 leads to the final BEXEA, which achieves the best results. Both arms contribute to offspring generation and solution space exploration. Although the searching behavior was not analyzed in this study, future work will focus on analyzing the searching behavior within the multi-armed adaptive search framework.

\subsubsection{Effectiveness of Explainable Uncertainty Criteria}
Another major component introduced in this work is the EU criteria, which assists in offspring sampling decisions, helping to select the best offspring for the expensive exact function evaluations. A detailed explanation of uncertainty and explainability is outlined in Section 3.3. In this section, we assess the effectiveness of the EU criteria proposed in this work. The EU criteria stem from EI criterion and introduce an entropy term to expand the information gathering process when deciding which candidates to select for evaluations. We compare it with the variant BEXEA-PF, which only includes the basic surrogate model prediction result as the sampling strategy, and BEXEA-EU, which only employs the proposed EU infill criteria as the sampling strategy. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed EU criteria. The combination of the EU infill criteria and the surrogate model prediction (PF) in a well-designed manner helps BEXEA achieve strong optimization performance with fewer true function evaluations while maintaining good convergence accuracy. The additional benefit of the EU criteria extends beyond its performance---it provides explainable uncertainty. This is especially useful in situations where the decision-making process needs to be more transparent, such as in clinical decision-making and healthcare institutions.

\subsection{Parameter Analysis}
We introduce two main parameters in BEXEA: the decay factor $\beta$ in the multi-armed bandit process, and the number of neighbors $\mathcal{K}$ in the mechanism of the EU criteria. The decay factor $\beta$ helps quantify the importance of the immediate reward relative to the future reward, which is essential in the environment-driven search process. The parameter $\mathcal{K}$ in the EU criteria determines how many neighbors are considered when estimating the predicted value and uncertainty. To study the influence of these parameters on BEXEA performance, we conduct a parameter analysis using the IEEE CEC 2022 test suite with twenty independent runs. For the setting of the parameter $\mathcal{K}$, we consider values below 20 for time efficiency. Based on the experimental results, we make several observations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The decay factor $\beta$ is set to 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, and 0.9. The best performance is achieved when the decay factor equals 0.3. Thus, in this study, we recommend setting this parameter to 0.3. A brief plot illustrating the experimental results across these parameter settings, including the convergence plot and the boxplot, is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:bexea_Beta}. We acknowledge the limitation of manually setting this parameter, and in future studies, we will explore making this parameter adaptive.
    \item The number of neighbors $\mathcal{K}$ is set to 4, 8, 12, 16, and 20. The best and most stable performance is observed when $\mathcal{K}$ is set to 8, as shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:bexea_K}. Therefore, we recommend setting this parameter to 8 in this study. While we recognize the limitation of manually setting this parameter, this will be addressed in future work.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Oil Reservoir Production Optimization}
We applied the proposed BEXEA to the oil reservoir production optimization problem to further validate its optimization advantage. The goal of this problem is to maximize production efficiency while minimizing operational costs. Specifically, the problem involves injection wells and production wells over the reservoir, and we aim to find the optimal control parameters for the injection and production rates at these wells over time, with the objective of maximizing the net present value (NPV). The injection wells inject water, and the production wells produce oil. Typically, the oil reservoir production optimization can be formulated as follows:

\begin{equation}
    \text{max} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \Delta t \cdot \left( r_o \cdot Q_{o,t}(\boldsymbol{X}) - r_w \cdot Q_{w,t}(\boldsymbol{X}) - r_i \cdot Q_{i,t}(\boldsymbol{X}) \right)
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{X}$ is the decision matrix of size equal to the number of wells multiplied by the number of time steps. In this work, we adopted the Egg model \cite{jansen2014egg}, which includes eight water injection wells and four production wells, resulting in 40 decision variables to be optimized. The lower and upper bounds of each decision variable are set to 0 STB/day and 500 STB/day, respectively. $\Delta t$ represents the time step, which is set to 720 in this study. $r_o$, $r_w$, and $r_i$ represent the oil production revenue, water production cost, and water injection cost, respectively. In this study, we set them to 20 USD/STB, 1 USD/STB, and 3 USD/STB, respectively. $Q_{o,t}$, $Q_{w,t}$, and $Q_{i,t}$ represent the oil production rate, water production rate, and water injection rate at time step $t$, respectively. These rates are calculated by a computationally expensive simulator.

The comparison peers used in this section are the same as those in the benchmark validation section. In this experiment, we set the maximum number of function evaluations to 200, with an initial sample size of 100. Each algorithm was run independently for 5 trials. The Egg model in this experiment was implemented using the MRST toolbox \cite{lie2019introduction}. The experimental results are presented in Table~\ref{t:bexea_Oil_Exp_Table}, which includes the average (Avg) result, standard deviation (Std) for the 5 independent runs, as well as the worst and best results. Additionally, we provide the average running time for each algorithm. From the table, we can see that the proposed BEXEA achieved the best result compared to the peers with less running time.

We also provide the visualization results of the NPV value in Fig.~\ref{fig:bexea_Oil_Exp_Figure}, which illustrates the optimization process of the oil reservoir production optimization. From the figure, it is evident that the proposed BEXEA outperforms the seven state-of-the-art SAEAs in optimizing the oil reservoir production problem.

\section{Conclusion}
In this study, we proposed the explainable uncertainty criteria-assisted bandit-driven adaptive search SAEA (BEXEA), aimed at improving the sampling trade-off between exploration and exploitation, as well as the explainability of selected candidate solutions. The proposed method outperforms state-of-the-art SAEAs in terms of optimization performance. The advantages of the proposed method were validated through numerical experiments using the recently released IEEE CEC 2022 test suite, as well as a commonly used benchmark set from the IEEE CEC 2005 in SAEAs. Furthermore, the effectiveness of the proposed MAB strategy and the EU criteria was validated, and the contribution of these components was verified through numerical experiments.

In the future, we plan to address the limitations of this study by enhancing the reward strategy used in the MAB framework, potentially by introducing group relative policy optimization (GRPO). Additionally, we aim to make the parameter in the EU criteria, specifically the neighborhood size used to calculate the entropy, dynamically adjustable based on the environment of the current candidate solutions. Since the EU criteria involve nearest-neighbor search and entropy calculation, which introduce additional computational overhead, we will also explore strategies to mitigate the impact of this increased runtime cost, ensuring a balance between accuracy and efficiency. Further research will explore applying the proposed BEXEA to multi-objective optimization problems and other real-world engineering optimization problems.
